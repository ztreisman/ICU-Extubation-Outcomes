\subsection{Unit clinical profiles}

The nine recognized ICU units fall into four clinically distinct groups which are used for a stratified Propensity Score Matching:

\textbf{Post-cardiac surgery (CVICU, $N=2{,}585$).} Oldest patients (mean
66.5y), 84\% circulatory diagnoses, short ventilation (55h mean), lowest FE
rate (1.7\%), best survival (80\%). Extubation is a near-routine step in
surgical recovery. This is the group where caregiver FE rate predicts survival.

\textbf{Medical critical care (MICU + MICU/SICU, $N=4{,}537$).} Highest SOFA
(8.4 and 7.6), longest ventilation (118h and 111h), 88\%+ medical-respiratory
intubation. MICU dominated by infectious (28\%) and respiratory (20\%) diagnoses;
MICU/SICU by neoplasms (16\%), infectious (27\%), and respiratory (19\%).
12-month survival 42\% and 41\%. No caregiver FE rate signal detected.

\textbf{Surgical and trauma (SICU + TSICU, $N=3{,}801$).} TSICU: youngest
(58.7y), lowest Charlson (4.3), 46\% injury/poisoning diagnoses, 63\% survival.
SICU: more heterogeneous (17.9\% digestive, 15.5\% injury, 11.2\% neoplasms),
53\% survival. Short-term mortality reversal observed in this group (see
Section~\ref{sec:surg_reversal}).

\textbf{Cardiac (CCU, $N=851$).} Oldest alongside CVICU (67.6y), highest
Charlson (7.0), highest norepinephrine use (0.25), 64\% circulatory
diagnoses. 37\% 12-month survival despite only 40.8\% hospital mortality ---
substantial post-discharge mortality. Excluded from stratified PSM due to
small caregiver count in the explicit analytical cohort (4 caregivers with
$n>10$ events), insufficient for quartile comparison.

\textbf{Neuro units ($N=766$).} Neuro SICU: 52.6\% hospital mortality despite
low SOFA (4.4) --- neurological injury severity is poorly captured by SOFA.
Neuro Intermediate and Stepdown have better survival (72\% and 71\%) but are
step-down rather than full ICU settings.

\section{Propensity Score Matching (PSM) and Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW)}

The propensity score model for each unit group includes: age, sex, Charlson
comorbidity index, first-day SOFA score, norepinephrine equivalent dose,
ventilation hours, intubation type (surgical / medical-respiratory /
medical-non-respiratory), and 21 binary diagnosis flags derived from ICD-10
categories. MIMIC-IV does not include a pre-computed APACHE score.

Figure~\ref{fig:love_cvicu} shows covariate balance before and after
matching in the CVICU group. All standardized mean differences fall
below 0.10 after matching.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../figures/psm_love_cvicu.png}
\caption{Covariate balance before and after PSM --- CVICU.}
\label{fig:love_cvicu}
\end{figure}

The analysis compares patients of top-quartile FE rate caregivers to
bottom-quartile FE rate caregivers, within each unit group. For PSM the matching is
1:1 nearest-neighbor within a 0.2 SD caliper on the logit propensity score,
with exact matching on unit within multi-unit groups. IPW uses the
Average Treatment Effect (ATE) estimand as a sensitivity check. In this context the "treatment" is being a patient of a high-FE-rate caregiver vs a low-FE-rate caregiver. The ATE answers: if we randomly assigned every patient in the population to either a high-FE or low-FE caregiver, what would the average difference in survival be?

The global mixed effects model is null ($p = 0.539$, P($<0$) = 70.7\%).
The unit-level random effect absorbs the survival differences across units,

CVICU-specific.

\section{Deep Learning Models}

A Python pipeline trains three models on the same patient-level covariates
used in the R analyses (70/15/15 train/val/test split, seed 237): L2-regularized
logistic regression, gradient-boosted trees (GBM), and a multilayer perceptron
(MLP) with entity embeddings for ICU unit, intubation type, primary diagnosis,
and sex. These are predictive benchmarks, not causal models. They ask whether
flexible nonlinear interactions improve discrimination and whether caregiver FE
rate contributes to prediction once the network can exploit richer feature
interactions.

\subsection{Predictive performance}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
Model & AUC-ROC & AUC-PR \\
\midrule
Logistic regression & 0.806 & 0.935 \\
Gradient boosting   & 0.828 & 0.943 \\
MLP (val, best ep.\ 16) & $\approx$0.825 & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The MLP matches gradient boosting closely (val AUC $\approx$0.825 at
early-stopping epoch 16), indicating no deep nonlinear structure beyond what
the tree ensemble already captures at this sample size ($N \approx 4{,}300$).
All models substantially outperform the survival-rate baseline (0.77 precision

Charlson, and ICU unit predict outcomes well.

\subsection{SHAP feature importance}

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../python/figures/shap_bar.png}
\caption{Top 20 features by mean $|$SHAP value$|$ from the MLP (200 test-set

\label{fig:shap_bar}

The SHAP ranking (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_bar}) is consistent with the R
analyses. Charlson comorbidity index is the dominant predictor by a wide
margin; \texttt{first\_careunit} (ICU unit embedding) ranks second --- confirming
that unit-level case-mix differences are the primary driver of survival
heterogeneity. Clinical severity variables (primary diagnosis, SOFA, age,
ventilation hours) follow. Caregiver FE rate ranks 10th out of 20, with a
negative direction in the SHAP dependence plot: higher FE rate pushes
predicted survival downward, consistent with the R PSM result. Its modest
rank relative to Charlson and unit reflects the same phenomenon observed in
the mixed-effects models --- FE rate carries real signal within units but is
dominated at the global level by case-mix confounding.

\subsection{Double/Debiased ML (DML)}

The partially linear DML estimator (Chernozhukov et al.\ 2018) uses 5-fold
cross-fitting with GBM nuisance models to partial out all measured confounders,
then regresses the survival residual on the FE rate residual:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Estimator & $\hat{\theta}$ & 95\% CI & $p$ \\
\midrule
DML (global, all units) & $-0.172$ & --- & $0.581$ \\
glmmTMB (global mixed effects) & $-0.554$ (log-odds) & --- & $0.539$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The DML $\hat{\theta}$ is on the probability scale: a one-unit
increase in FE rate (0$\to$1, a theoretical maximum) would reduce
$P(\text{survival})$ by 17.2 percentage points. Over the observed IQR
($\approx$5.5 pp of FE rate), the implied survival difference is $<$1 pp ---
small and not significant ($p = 0.581$). This global null is consistent with
the R glmmTMB result ($p = 0.539$) and with the PSM finding that the signal is
CVICU-specific: pooling all units, the negative effect in CVICU is diluted by
the null in Medical and Surgical/Trauma groups.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.80\textwidth]{../python/figures/dml_caregiver_fe.png}
\caption{DML partialled-out scatter (left) and treatment residual distribution
(right). The negative slope ($\hat{\theta} = -0.172$, $p = 0.581$) is

\label{fig:dml}
\end{figure}

\noindent In summary, the deep learning pipeline corroborates the R analysis:
the direction of the FE rate effect is consistently negative, the global signal
is null once confounders are removed, and the dominant predictors of survival
are patient severity and ICU unit rather than caregiver practice.


\section{Trajectory Analysis}

Andrew's proposed dynamic risk framework requires that reintubation risk
be estimable as a function of time on the ventilator, so that the optimal
extubation time can be identified as the point where one more day of waiting
produces less than approximately 0.5\% reduction in failure probability.
We tested whether two standard readiness variables --- RSBI and P/F ratio
--- carry the trajectory information this framework requires.

\subsection{Data}

RSBI (spontaneous respiratory rate / spontaneous tidal volume in liters)
and P/F ratio (PaO$_2$/FiO$_2$) were pulled from MIMIC-IV for all explicit
extubation events in the \texttt{all\_extubations} cohort (6,063 events,
5,505 patients). The outcome is \texttt{failed\_extubation\_flag}: reintubation
within 72 hours of this specific extubation event. This is the per-event
outcome, not the patient-lifetime reintubation count.

\midrule

\bottomrule

\end{center}

\noindent RSBI level at extubation is a significant predictor ($p = 0.032$);
neither slope is significant ($p = 0.634$ and $p = 0.726$). The trajectory
of RSBI or P/F ratio in the 72 hours before extubation carries no independent
predictive information beyond the level at the moment of extubation.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The null slope finding is consistent with two non-exclusive explanations.
First, RSBI and P/F ratio may not be sensitive enough variables to detect
the trajectory signal --- they are measured infrequently (median 4--6
measurements per patient in the 72h window) and are inherently noisy.
Second, clinicians may already be conditioning on trajectory implicitly ---
extubating only after the patient has plateaued at a low RSBI --- so that
by the time of extubation the trajectory information is spent.

The dynamic risk framework remains theoretically sound. Implementing it
prospectively would require variables with higher temporal resolution than
are available in retrospective EHR data: continuous pressure support level,
FiO$_2$ weaning trajectory, and spontaneous breathing trial (SBT) outcomes
are candidates. Retrospective validation from MIMIC-IV data alone is not
feasible with current measurement density.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]

\item \textbf{Caregiver attribution.} \texttt{caregiver\_id} records the
documenting user. This likely attenuates the signal --- a noisy proxy for the
performing provider would dilute a real caregiver effect.

\item \textbf{Single center.} MIMIC-IV is from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical
Center. An eICU parallel analysis (208 ICUs, with caregiver type information)
is planned to assess generalizability.

\item \textbf{Unmeasured confounding.} PSM controls only for measured
covariates. Shift patterns, time of day, attending supervision, and respiratory
therapist involvement are not captured in MIMIC-IV.

\item \textbf{All-cause 12-month mortality.} Deaths unrelated to the index
extubation are counted. Shorter-term outcomes (hospital mortality, 30-day)
are included as secondary endpoints.

\item \textbf{Missed extubation opportunities.} Patients extubated too late
cannot be identified in retrospective data without knowing the counterfactual.
This is the motivation for the dynamic risk analysis (Andrew's Analysis 2).

\end{enumerate}
